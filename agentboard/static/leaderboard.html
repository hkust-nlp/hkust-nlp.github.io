We conduct a comprehensive, multi-turn LLM agent evaluation of popular large language models, including both API-based closed-source models and open-source models and analyze the performance through main results, dimension score, reward-step score, and scores at various levels of difficulty.

Main Performance

Dataset Statistics: We categorize the nine tasks into four distinct categories: Embodied AI, Games, Web, and Tools. Under Embodied AI, we include Alfworld, ScienceWorld, and BabyAI; in the Game category, we have Jericho and PDDL; the Web category encompasses WebShop and WebArena; and finally, for Tools, we include Query-Tool and Operation-Tool.
Evaluation metrics: We introduce two primary metrics for evaluation: success rateand progress rate. The success rate measures the proportion of instances in which the goal of an environment is achieved. The progress rate reflects the proportion of completed sub-goals. grounding score

Dimensional Analysis

Our dimension scoring framework evaluates a model's capability across six dimensions: memory, planning, world modeling, retrospection, grounding, and spatial navigation.

  • GPT-4 exhibits superior performance across all dimensions, significantly surpassing other LLMs.
  • Generally, closed-source LLMs outperform open-source models comprehensively and exhibit balanced abilities.
  • Open-sourced LLMs exhibit weaknesses in self-reflection, world modeling, and planning.

Reward-step Analysis

Here shows how the model's reward score changes with the number of steps. We observe that closed-sourced models (i.e. GPT-4, Claude2 and GPT-3.5-Turbo) continue to gain rewards across 30 steps in tasks of Alfworld and PDDL. In contrast, in Webshop and Tool, they rapidly reach a peak reward value and then cease to gain further rewards. This trend may be due to the fact that tasks in Embodied AI and Games generally require more steps to complete. However, for categories like Web and Tools, the tasks require fewer steps to complete, leading to a quicker encounter with subgoals that these models are unable to solve.
For open-sourced models, they quickly reach their peak reward score. As the number of steps increases, they fail to continue gaining rewards in all tasks. This may indicate their limited ability to handle long-range interactions. This suggests that these models struggle with longer steps due to the corresponding increase in context length, hindering their ability to effectively complete subgoals.

Difficulty Analysis

For each of the task, we divide the environments into "easy" and "hard" two categories. These environments are divided according to the task types or the number of subgoals.
Notably, even with a minor difference in task complexity (for some tasks, easy samples consisting of fewer than 3 subgoals, and hard samples predominantly comprising 4-6 subgoals), all models record a considerable performance decline, with GPT4 success rate experiencing an average drop of 28.4%, which indicated that even the most robust Language Learning Models (LLM) such as GPT-4 are limited in terms of task compositionality.
